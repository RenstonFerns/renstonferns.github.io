This seminar presents my research on the techno-economic assessment of an integrated e-methanol production plant utilizing first- and second-generation biomass resources. The work focuses on how biomass, captured CO₂, and hydrogen-assisted methanol synthesis can be connected into a circular carbon fuel pathway for sustainable liquid fuel production.

The talk is based on my SAE Technical Paper:

**Techno-Economic Analysis of an Integrated E-Methanol Production Plant Utilizing First and Second Generation Biomass Resources**  
SAE Technical Paper 2025-01-0182  
DOI: [https://doi.org/10.4271/2025-01-0182](https://doi.org/10.4271/2025-01-0182)

## Talk Overview

The seminar discusses an integrated circular carbon fuel system that combines:

- biomass-to-ethanol fermentation,
- biogenic CO₂ capture,
- biomass residue gasification,
- syngas-to-methanol conversion,
- CO₂ hydrogenation to e-methanol,
- and techno-economic assessment for regional deployment.

The study evaluates two biomass pathways:

1. **Sugarcane-based e-methanol production in Brazil**
2. **Sugar beet-based e-methanol production in France**

The broader goal is to understand whether existing agricultural and bioethanol infrastructure can be extended toward circular carbon methanol production while improving biomass utilization, CO₂ utilization, and economic feasibility.

## Background {#background}

The transport sector remains one of the major contributors to global greenhouse gas emissions. While electrification is central to transport decarbonization, sustainable liquid fuels remain important for applications where energy density, infrastructure compatibility, storage, and long-distance operation matter.

E-methanol is gaining attention as a low-carbon liquid fuel and chemical platform. However, its economic feasibility depends strongly on regional resources, electricity prices, hydrogen cost, carbon policy, feedstock availability, and plant operating conditions.

This work examines whether biomass-derived carbon, captured CO₂, and hydrogen-assisted methanol synthesis can be integrated into a circular fuel system using first- and second-generation biomass resources.

## Integrated E-Methanol Pathway {#integrated-e-methanol-pathway}

The integrated pathway combines first-generation biomass conversion with second-generation residue valorization.

In the first stage, sugar-rich biomass such as sugarcane or sugar beet is converted into ethanol through fermentation. This step produces a concentrated biogenic CO₂ stream, which can be captured and reused for e-methanol synthesis.

In parallel, biomass residues such as bagasse, pulp, leaves, or other lignocellulosic fractions are gasified to produce syngas. This syngas can then be converted into methanol through catalytic synthesis.

The integrated concept can be simplified as:

Sugarcane / Sugar Beet
        ↓
Ethanol Fermentation  →  Bioethanol + Biogenic CO₂
                                      ↓
                              CO₂ Capture
                                      ↓
Residue Gasification  →  Syngas  →  Methanol Synthesis
                                      ↓
                       Integrated E-Methanol Production

This system is designed to improve carbon utilization by using both the main biomass feedstock and the associated residues.

## Techno-Economic Assessment Framework {#techno-economic-assessment-framework}

The techno-economic assessment evaluates whether the integrated e-methanol production system can become economically meaningful under different regional and market conditions.

The assessment includes:

* capital expenditure estimation,
* operational expenditure estimation,
* feedstock cost assessment,
* electricity cost assessment,
* carbon credit assumptions,
* levelized cost of methanol calculation,
* net present value analysis,
* internal rate of return,
* payback period,
* and scenario-based sensitivity analysis.

The key economic metric used in the study is the **Levelized Cost of Methanol (LCOM)**, which estimates the break-even methanol production cost over the plant lifetime.

## Scenarios Evaluated {#scenarios-evaluated}

The seminar discusses three main techno-economic cases:

### 1. Base Case

The base case uses region-specific default assumptions for feedstock price, electricity price, operating hours, and carbon credit values.

### 2. Low-Carbon Electricity Scenario

This scenario replaces grid electricity with low-carbon electricity through off-site power purchase agreements. This is especially important because hydrogen production through electrolysis dominates the electricity demand of the integrated system.

### 3. Feedstock Price Sensitivity

This scenario evaluates how optimistic, baseline, and pessimistic feedstock prices affect LCOM, NPV, and overall economic feasibility.

The study also considers carbon credit assumptions to evaluate how policy mechanisms can influence the commercial outlook of circular carbon methanol systems.

## Key Findings {#key-findings}

The sugarcane-based system in Brazil shows stronger economic potential than the sugar beet-based system in France.

Under the base case:

* the sugarcane pathway achieved an LCOM of approximately **$0.493/kg MeOH**,
* the sugar beet pathway achieved an LCOM of approximately **$2.42/kg MeOH**.

The sugarcane case benefited from lower electricity cost, longer annual operating hours, and better feedstock availability. The sugar beet case was economically constrained by higher electricity prices, seasonal feedstock availability, and shorter operating duration.

Under the low-carbon electricity scenario using off-site power purchase agreements:

* the sugarcane pathway improved to approximately **$0.36/kg MeOH**,
* the sugarcane case achieved a positive NPV,
* and the payback period was approximately **2.8 years**.

The sugar beet case improved under low-carbon electricity assumptions but remained economically challenging due to regional cost and operating constraints.

## Why This Work Matters {#why-this-work-matters}

This work shows that e-methanol production cannot be evaluated only from a process chemistry perspective. Regional deployment matters.

The feasibility of circular carbon e-methanol depends on:

* feedstock price,
* electricity cost,
* hydrogen production cost,
* carbon credit value,
* operating hours,
* process integration,
* biomass residue availability,
* and policy support.

The study highlights the importance of connecting process modelling with techno-economic assessment to identify where e-fuel systems may become viable and where additional innovation, policy support, or infrastructure development is needed.

For emerging fuel pathways, this kind of assessment supports early-stage decision-making before large-scale deployment.
